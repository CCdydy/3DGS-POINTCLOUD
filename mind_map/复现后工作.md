# LiDAR compression for 3DGS reconstruction: an open research gap

> 注：这份文档当前是英文研究定位稿，用于提炼 problem statement、related work 入口和投稿叙事，
> 不是逐日实验日志。实验执行流程请看根目录 `REPRODUCE.md`。

**No published work connects LiDAR point cloud compression to 3D Gaussian Splatting rendering quality for autonomous driving.** This gap sits at a well-defined intersection of three mature but siloed communities: LiDAR compression (which evaluates only D1/D2 geometry PSNR), driving-scene 3DGS (which assumes raw, uncompressed LiDAR), and rendering-aware point cloud coding (which handles only dense RGB objects, not sparse outdoor LiDAR). The absence of even a basic empirical "sensitivity curve" mapping LiDAR compression bitrate to downstream rendering quality (camera PSNR, SSIM, LPIPS) makes this a clean, defensible, and timely research entry point — particularly given that autonomous driving simulation increasingly relies on 3DGS-based neural rendering of sensor data.

---

## 1. Confirmed gap statement

No existing paper simultaneously (a) compresses LiDAR point clouds from autonomous driving scenes and (b) uses 3D Gaussian Splatting rendering quality as either an optimization objective or an evaluation metric. No paper establishes the empirical relationship between LiDAR compression bitrate (BPP) or geometry distortion (D1/D2 PSNR) and downstream 3DGS rendering metrics (camera PSNR, SSIM, LPIPS) — the "sensitivity curve" that would quantify how much rendering quality degrades as compression increases. The closest work, Bits-to-Photon (arXiv:2406.05915), demonstrates end-to-end learned compression optimized for Gaussian splatting rendering, but operates exclusively on **dense, colorful indoor point clouds** (8iVFB, THuman 2.0 human bodies) and does not address sparse LiDAR geometry, outdoor scenes, or driving-specific challenges. Meanwhile, all LiDAR compression papers (RENO, G-PCC, ELiC, OctSqueeze, Unicorn) optimize cross-entropy or occupancy losses and evaluate with D1/D2 PSNR exclusively, while all driving-scene 3DGS methods (Street Gaussians, SplatAD, DrivingGaussian, TCLC-GS) consume raw uncompressed LiDAR without ever studying compression effects.

## 3. How Street Gaussians uses LiDAR — and why compression matters

Street Gaussians (ECCV 2024) replaces the SfM point cloud used by vanilla 3DGS with **aggregated LiDAR sweeps from the ego vehicle**, specifically because SfM fails in textureless and under-observed regions common in urban driving. The initialization pipeline works as follows: foreground points inside tracked 3D bounding boxes are extracted into per-object local coordinates; the remaining background cloud is **voxel-downsampled at 0.15 m** and visibility-filtered; each LiDAR point receives an RGB color by projecting it onto the nearest camera image; SfM points from COLMAP supplement distant regions beyond LiDAR range. The paper's ablation (Table 5) shows LiDAR and SfM are complementary — LiDAR better models foreground objects while SfM better captures background structure.

The broader literature on 3DGS initialization sensitivity reveals that **spatial accuracy, scene coverage, and noise level** are the three most critical properties of the input point cloud. RAIN-GS (NeurIPS 2024) demonstrated that noisy initialization causes "limited Gaussian transportability" — Gaussians overfit at incorrect positions rather than migrating to correct locations. LIVE-GS/GSFusion showed that sparse LiDAR regions produce overly large, blurry Gaussians. S3R-GS found that LiDAR's limited vertical field-of-view degrades initialization for tall structures. These findings collectively suggest that lossy LiDAR compression — which introduces geometric noise, reduces point density, and can distort local structure — would systematically degrade 3DGS rendering quality, but **nobody has measured this**.

## 4. Lessons from image compression's paradigm shift

The image compression community traversed a well-documented trajectory from PSNR-optimized to perceptual and task-optimized coding over 2017–2024. **3D point cloud compression is approximately 5–7 years behind this curve.** The key milestones provide a direct roadmap:

Ballé et al. (ICLR 2017, arXiv:1611.01704) introduced end-to-end learned image compression, still MSE-optimized but using nonlinear transforms that naturally reduced perceptual artifacts. Zhang et al. (CVPR 2018) then provided LPIPS as a differentiable perceptual metric, which became the standard training loss for perceptual codecs. Blau & Michaeli (ICML 2019, arXiv:1901.07821) formalized the **rate-distortion-perception tradeoff**, proving mathematically that distortion and perceptual quality are fundamentally at odds. Mentzer et al. (NeurIPS 2020) combined GANs with learned compression in HiFiC, achieving user-preferred quality at half the bitrate of BPG. In parallel, Duan et al. (IEEE TIP 2020) launched Video Coding for Machines (VCM), extending R-D optimization to include downstream task accuracy: minimize R + λ_D·D + λ_T·T_loss.

In the 3D domain, analogous developments are nascent. Quach et al. (2021, arXiv:2102.12839) proposed a deep perceptual metric for point cloud geometry — the closest analog to LPIPS — but it has not been widely adopted. PCQA metrics like GraphSIM and PCQM exist but are rarely used in compression optimization loops. Task-aware PCC papers (RPCGC, suLPCC, PC4M) target detection and segmentation — **none target rendering**. The R-D-P tradeoff has not been formally applied to 3D compression. This maturity gap creates a clear opportunity: applying the rendering-as-downstream-task paradigm (analogous to VCM) to LiDAR compression is both theoretically motivated and practically novel.

## 5. SOTA LiDAR compression anchors and their evaluation blind spots

**RENO** (CVPR 2025, arXiv:2503.12382) is the current best neural LiDAR codec, achieving **12.25% BD-Rate savings** over G-PCCv23 and **48.34%** over Draco at real-time speeds (10 fps on RTX 3090). Its loss function is pure cross-entropy over octree occupancy codes: L = E[−log P_θ(O)]. It evaluates with D1 PSNR, D2 PSNR, BPP, and downstream 3D object detection via CenterPoint on KITTI and Ford datasets. Code is open at github.com/NJUVISION/RENO.

**G-PCC v23** (TMC13) remains the universal benchmark anchor, available at github.com/MPEGGroup/mpeg-pcc-tmc13. Every neural LiDAR codec paper since 2023 reports BD-Rate savings against G-PCCv23.

A critical observation: **every 2025 LiDAR compression paper** — RENO, ELiC (arXiv:2511.14070), TopNet (CVPR 2025), LINR-PCGC (ICCV 2025), the generalized Gaussian entropy model (CVPR 2025) — evaluates exclusively with D1/D2 PSNR. Multiple survey papers explicitly note this limitation. The ACM TOMM 2025 survey states that D1/D2 metrics "may not be suitable for all applications such as LiDAR point clouds for autonomous driving which may require application-specific distortion metrics." The Frontiers 2022 survey identifies an open question: "how to best exploit such characteristics of compression algorithms for rendering remains unexplored."

## 6. Proposed starting point: the sensitivity-curve paper

Given the confirmed gap, the most natural, defensible research entry point is an **empirical sensitivity study** — not modifying the compressor initially, but characterizing the problem first. The concrete proposal:

**Phase 1 — Establish the sensitivity curve (the paper's core contribution).** Take a standard driving dataset with LiDAR and multi-view images (Waymo Open, nuScenes, or KITTI-360). Compress the aggregated LiDAR point clouds at multiple quality levels using G-PCCv23 and RENO (both have open code). Feed each compressed output through the Street Gaussians pipeline as initialization. Measure rendering quality (PSNR, SSIM, LPIPS) across bitrate operating points. Plot the BPP → rendering quality curve and the D1/D2 → rendering quality correlation. This experiment alone answers the question: *does optimizing D1/D2 PSNR actually optimize rendering quality, or is there a mismatch?*

**Why pre-processing/analysis first, not modifying the compressor.** Three reasons. First, the sensitivity curve itself is a novel contribution — it has never been published, and the community needs this empirical foundation before any optimization work is credible. Second, modifying RENO's loss function to include a differentiable rendering loss requires backpropagating through both the compression decoder and the entire 3DGS training loop, which is computationally prohibitive without first knowing which distortion modes matter most (is it point displacement? density reduction? local structure distortion?). Third, an arXiv-first exploration paper gains maximum citation value by defining the problem space rather than proposing a narrow solution.

**Phase 2 (optional extension).** Based on Phase 1 findings, design a lightweight **rendering-aware post-processing or re-weighting** module that sits between the LiDAR decoder and the 3DGS initializer. This avoids modifying the codec's internals while still optimizing for downstream rendering — analogous to how early VCM work added task-aware post-filters before modifying the core codec. Specific candidates include: (a) density-adaptive voxel resampling tuned for Gaussian initialization scale, (b) rendering-importance-weighted denoising that prioritizes geometric accuracy in regions most visible to training cameras, or (c) a learned point-cloud refinement network trained with a differentiable 3DGS rendering loss.

## 8. Risk assessment

| Source of risk | Assessment | Reasoning |
|----------------|------------|-----------|
| **NJU Vision Lab** (RENO authors) | **MODERATE** | They published RENO (LiDAR compression) and a separate 3DGS compression paper (VCIP 2024). They have all the components in-house. However, as of April 2026, they have not connected these two lines of work. |
| **Zenseact** (SplatAD authors) | LOW | SplatAD renders both camera and LiDAR from 3DGS but has no compression angle. Their focus is simulation fidelity, not bandwidth. |
| **Waymo / Google** | LOW | Moving toward generative world models (Genie 3), not compression-based reconstruction. |
| **NVIDIA / Meta** | LOW | Focused on 3DGS compression for VR/streaming (CodecGS, CompGS), not LiDAR input optimization. |
| **MPEG/VCM community** | LOW | VCM standardization focuses on 2D video; PCC evaluation standards still use D1/D2 exclusively. |
| **Concurrent arXiv preprints** | LOW | Exhaustive search of 2025–early 2026 preprints reveals no paper bridging LiDAR compression → 3DGS rendering. |

**Overall scooping risk: LOW to MODERATE.** The gap is genuine, well-defined, and unfilled. The primary risk vector is the NJU Vision lab, which possesses expertise in both domains. Speed to publication matters — an arXiv-first paper establishing the sensitivity curve would stake the claim effectively. The empirical, problem-characterization nature of the proposed paper also provides natural defensibility: even if someone concurrently proposes a rendering-aware LiDAR codec, the foundational sensitivity analysis remains independently valuable and citable.

## 9. Conclusion: a well-timed intersection paper

The research opportunity is unusually clean. Three mature subfields — LiDAR compression, 3DGS-based driving simulation, and perceptual/task-aware coding — have developed in parallel without anyone bridging the specific connection between LiDAR compression quality and neural rendering quality in driving scenes. The image compression community's 2017–2024 transition from PSNR to perceptual and task-aware optimization provides both theoretical motivation (rate-distortion-perception tradeoff) and a proven methodological template (VCM's joint R-D-T optimization). The fact that all necessary tools are open-source — RENO and G-PCCv23 for compression, Street Gaussians and SplatAD for rendering, standard driving datasets for evaluation — means the sensitivity-curve experiment is immediately executable. This positions the proposed paper not as an incremental improvement to any single method, but as the first empirical bridge between two communities, with a clear follow-up path toward rendering-aware LiDAR codec design.
